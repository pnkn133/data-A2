Income Is Not the Same Everywhere

The national debate about university value starts from an uneven income landscape. In 2022-23, median personal income was highest in the ACT and Northern Territory, while Tasmania and South Australia sat lower.

The Income Benchmark Is Higher in Some Places

Ranking the same 2022-23 median income values makes the gap easier to compare. The ACT, Northern Territory, and Western Australia sit above the national median, while Tasmania is furthest below it.

The Benchmark Has Been Moving Up

Median income rose in every state and territory between 2018-19 and 2022-23. This matters for the university question because graduate salaries need to be compared with a baseline that is also rising.

Study Is Concentrated Before Age 25

Education participation is highest among teenagers and people in their early twenties, then falls quickly across every state and territory. This places the university decision early in adult life, before many people reach their highest earning years.

Study Often Happens Alongside Work

Studying is not only a full-time student experience. In 2025, part-time employed and unemployed Australians were much more likely to be currently studying than full-time employed Australians, showing how education often overlaps with labour-market transition.

Degree Completion Shows Up Most in Mid-Career Ages

The completed degree profile is different from the study participation profile. Bachelor degree attainment is lower in the early twenties, then rises sharply for people aged 25 to 44, where completed qualifications have had time to accumulate.

Graduate Employment Weakened After 2023

Graduate outcomes remain strong, especially for postgraduate coursework graduates, but the 2024 survey shows a softer full-time employment result across undergraduate, postgraduate coursework, and postgraduate research graduates.

Some Fields Convert to Full-Time Work Faster

The graduate employment payoff is not uniform across degrees. In 2024, rehabilitation, pharmacy, medicine, teacher education, nursing, engineering, and dentistry sat well above the undergraduate average, while creative arts and communications were much lower.

Graduate Salaries Clear the National Benchmark

Salary is the clearest short-term payoff measure. Even undergraduate median full-time graduate salaries sat above the national median personal income benchmark, while postgraduate salaries were much higher again.

The Salary Premium Is Substantial

Expressing salary as a gap makes the payoff easier to read. In 2024, the undergraduate median full-time salary was about $16,800 above the national median, while postgraduate outcomes were more than $40,000 higher.

So, Is a University Degree Still Worth It?

Overall, the evidence suggests that a university degree still has value in Australia, especially when measured through graduate salaries and employment outcomes. Graduate median full-time salaries sit above the national median income, and some study areas, such as rehabilitation, pharmacy, medicine, nursing, teaching, engineering, and dentistry, convert strongly into full-time work.

However, the value of a degree is not automatic. Income differs across states, study participation is shaped by age and work status, and graduate outcomes vary heavily by field and qualification level. A degree can improve employment and earning prospects, but students still need to consider what they study, where they live, and whether the expected salary premium is worth the time and cost of higher education.

Data sources: Australian Bureau of Statistics Personal Income in Australia, ABS Education and Work, and QILT Graduate Outcomes Survey tables.
